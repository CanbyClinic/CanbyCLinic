Diabetes prevention

Prediabetes in the Valley: The Window Before Disease

Prediabetes is common, usually silent, and often missed. Screening can identify risk while prevention still has meaningful leverage.

Canby Community Clinic Health Education Team•July 15, 2026•12 min read
Medical information notice: This article is general education and does not diagnose or treat a medical condition. Screening and treatment decisions should be made with a qualified health professional who knows your history. Call 911 in an emergency.

Prediabetes is often described as a warning sign, but the phrase can be misleading. It is not a flashing alarm that creates symptoms. Most people feel no different. It is a laboratory-defined state in which blood glucose is higher than normal but has not reached the threshold for diabetes. Its importance lies in what it predicts: a greater chance of developing type 2 diabetes, heart disease, and stroke.

CDC materials updated in 2026 estimated that 115.2 million U.S. adults—more than two in five—had prediabetes and that roughly eight in ten did not know it. Large national estimates should not be used to label an individual. They show why risk-based screening matters: waiting for symptoms will miss many people during the period when prevention may be most effective.

Prediabetes is a category, not a destiny

Blood sugar exists on a continuum. Diagnostic thresholds create useful categories, but biology does not change suddenly at a single decimal point. A person just below a threshold may have similar risk to someone just above it. Results should be interpreted with age, weight history, pregnancy history, medications, family history, sleep, activity, and cardiovascular risk.

Prediabetes does not guarantee progression to diabetes. Some people remain stable, some return to a normal range, and some progress. The purpose of identifying it is to create a chance for effective action, not to assign blame.

Who should discuss screening

The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force recommends screening adults aged 35 to 70 who have overweight or obesity and offering or referring people with prediabetes to effective preventive interventions. It also notes that earlier screening may be considered for populations with disproportionately high diabetes prevalence and at a lower body-mass-index threshold for Asian American adults.

Guidelines are starting points. A clinician may consider family history, a history of gestational diabetes, polycystic ovary syndrome, cardiovascular disease, certain medications, or previous abnormal results. Symptoms such as excessive thirst, frequent urination, unexplained weight loss, or blurred vision require diagnostic evaluation rather than routine screening.

What the tests measure

Common tests include hemoglobin A1C, fasting plasma glucose, and the oral glucose tolerance test. A1C estimates average glucose exposure over roughly two to three months. Fasting glucose is a snapshot after a period without caloric intake. The oral glucose tolerance test shows how the body handles a measured glucose load.

Each test has limitations. A1C can be affected by anemia, hemoglobin variants, recent blood loss, kidney disease, pregnancy, and other conditions. Fasting status may be inaccurate. Acute illness can temporarily raise glucose. Abnormal results may need confirmation unless symptoms and values clearly establish diabetes. A patient should ask what test was used, whether it should be repeated, and what factors might affect interpretation.

The prevention evidence is behavioral—but not simplistic

Effective prevention programs commonly focus on sustainable nutrition patterns, regular physical activity, modest weight reduction when appropriate, sleep, and continuing support. This is not the same as telling a patient to “try harder.” Behavior is shaped by food cost, work schedules, neighborhood safety, stress, caregiving, disability, and access to culturally familiar options.

A plan must fit the patient’s life. Replacing one daily sugar-sweetened drink may be more realistic than an immediate total diet overhaul. Ten-minute walks after meals may be more sustainable than an unused gym membership. For someone who works nights, sleep planning may be as important as exercise advice. Small changes become powerful when they are repeated.

Weight is relevant, but shame is not treatment

Body weight is one risk factor among many. Genetics, age, pregnancy history, medication exposure, sleep, stress, and social conditions also matter. Shame can reduce follow-up and make honest conversations harder. A clinically useful discussion focuses on measurable actions, barriers, and progress rather than moral judgment.

Some people with prediabetes may be candidates for medication in addition to lifestyle intervention, depending on age, body mass index, pregnancy history, and other risk. That decision belongs in a clinical conversation. No supplement should be assumed to prevent diabetes simply because it is marketed for blood sugar.

Why follow-up is essential

Prediabetes is not managed by one educational handout. The patient needs to know when testing should be repeated, what target is being used, and how progress will be measured. Blood pressure, cholesterol, smoking, and other cardiovascular risks should also be addressed because diabetes risk is part of a broader metabolic picture.

Follow-up can identify both improvement and progression. If results worsen, earlier treatment may reduce complications. If results improve, the plan can be reinforced and adjusted. Without a scheduled next step, a “borderline” result can disappear into the record until symptoms develop years later.

A practical appointment checklist

  • Ask which glucose test was performed and what the exact result means.
  • Ask whether the result needs confirmation.
  • Bring a list of medications, including steroids and psychiatric medications that may affect glucose.
  • Discuss family history and any history of gestational diabetes.
  • Choose one or two realistic behavior changes and a method for tracking them.
  • Ask when repeat testing is recommended.
  • Review blood pressure, cholesterol, smoking, sleep, and other cardiovascular risks.

The window matters because disease is easier to prevent than reverse

Type 2 diabetes can lead to cardiovascular disease, kidney disease, nerve damage, vision problems, and other complications. Prevention is not guaranteed, but prediabetes provides information before those outcomes are inevitable. It creates a period in which patients and clinicians can reduce risk with more leverage than they may have after years of uncontrolled disease.

The most useful message is neither panic nor reassurance. Prediabetes is common, usually silent, and clinically meaningful. It deserves confirmation, a practical prevention plan, and follow-up. The diagnosis is not a verdict. It is a window—and windows are valuable only when someone acts while they are open.

Food advice must be specific enough to use

“Eat healthier” is not a treatment plan. Useful nutrition counseling starts with what the patient already eats, where food is purchased, who cooks, cultural preferences, budget, and work schedule. The same recommendation can be translated into many food traditions. The goal is not to replace identity with a standardized diet.

Practical changes may include reducing sugary drinks, increasing fiber, pairing carbohydrates with protein, adjusting portion size, or planning meals to reduce late-night convenience eating. The best first step is one the patient can repeat. A plan that is theoretically perfect but abandoned after a week has less value than a modest change sustained for months.

Sleep and stress are metabolic factors

Short or irregular sleep can affect appetite, activity, and glucose regulation. Chronic stress can shape eating patterns and make preventive routines harder to maintain. These factors are especially relevant for shift workers, caregivers, and people with multiple jobs.

Discussing sleep and stress does not mean diabetes risk is “all in the mind.” It recognizes that metabolism occurs inside a whole life. Screening creates an opportunity to identify these barriers early and choose interventions that fit the person rather than blame the person for not fitting the intervention.

Progress should be measured beyond the scale

Weight can be one useful outcome, but it is not the only one. Increased activity, improved sleep, lower blood pressure, better food quality, and consistent follow-up are meaningful. A patient may improve metabolic health before a major change appears on the scale.

Focusing only on weight can hide success and discourage people whose bodies respond differently. A broader scorecard makes the plan more durable: What behaviors became easier? What barrier was removed? Did laboratory values change? Is the patient able to continue without excessive cost or stress? Prevention works through patterns, and patterns deserve more than one measurement.
